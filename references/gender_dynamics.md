# Gender Dynamics Overlay

Same behavior, different perception. This module adjusts strategy recommendations
to account for documented gender biases in workplace communication.

**Important**: This module describes *observed patterns in how behavior is perceived*,
not how behavior *should* be perceived. The goal is to help users navigate reality
as it is while working toward how it should be.

---

## The Double Standard Matrix

Research consistently shows the same behavior is evaluated differently based on gender:

| Behavior | Perceived in men as | Perceived in women as |
|----------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Direct communication | Confident, decisive | Aggressive, abrasive |
| Asking for a raise | Ambitious, knows their worth | Greedy, ungrateful |
| Self-promotion | Leadership material | Boastful, unlikable |
| Showing emotion | Passionate, invested | Unstable, unprofessional |
| Being quiet in meetings | Thoughtful, strategic | Disengaged, lacking confidence |
| Taking charge | Natural leader | Bossy, controlling |
| Being collaborative | Team player | Not leadership material |
| Negotiating hard | Tough, skilled | Difficult, demanding |

## Strategy Adjustments

### For women navigating these biases

**Amplification strategy**
When you make a point in a meeting, have an ally repeat and credit it:
"As [your name] just said, [restate the point]. I think that's exactly right."
This prevents the idea from being attributed to someone else and reinforces your ownership.

**Strategic warmth buffering**
When you need to be direct (which you should be), add a single warmth signal
at the start — not to soften, but to prevent the "abrasive" misread:
- Instead of: "This approach won't work because..."
- Try: "I want to make sure we get this right. My concern is..."
The content is identical. The perception shifts significantly.

**Claim credit proactively, not reactively**
Don't wait for someone to steal your credit and then fight for it back.
Build visibility in advance:
- Send "progress update" emails that document your contributions
- Present your own work whenever possible (don't delegate the presentation)
- Use "I" language for individual contributions: "I designed the architecture for..."

**Negotiation framing**
Research shows women face backlash for negotiating *for themselves* but not
when negotiating *for their team or for fairness*:
- Instead of: "I want a raise because I deserve it"
- Try: "I want to make sure my compensation reflects the scope of my role,
  especially given the additional responsibilities I've taken on for the team"

### For men working with women colleagues

**Check attribution patterns**
Before presenting an idea, ask: "Whose idea was this originally?"
If the answer is a woman colleague, explicitly credit her.

**Watch for "office housework" imbalance**
Note-taking, scheduling, ordering food, planning events — these tasks
disproportionately fall to women. Be aware of this and volunteer to share them.

**Amplify, don't explain**
If a woman colleague makes a point, don't restate it in your own words
(which reads as claiming it). Instead: "I agree with what [name] said."

## Cross-Reference with Persona Types

The gender overlay MODIFIES but doesn't replace the persona type strategy.

**Example: D-type woman boss**
- Base DISC strategy says: "Be direct, get to the point"
- Gender overlay adds: "She may have developed extra-direct style to overcome
  softness bias. Don't mistake her directness for hostility — she's had to
  overcorrect. Match her directness."

**Example: High-Agreeableness man**
- Base Big Five strategy says: "He avoids conflict, seeks harmony"
- Gender overlay adds: "He may face pressure to be more assertive. His
  collaborative style might be undervalued by D-type leaders. He may
  overcompensate in high-stakes meetings."

**Example: Woman asking for raise from male boss**
- Base salary scenario applies
- Gender overlay adds: Frame around role scope and market data, not personal worth.
  If boss perceives the ask as "demanding," re-anchor: "I want to make sure the
  compensation reflects the responsibilities accurately. Here's the market data."

## Cultural × Gender Intersections

Gender dynamics vary significantly across cultures:

**Chinese workplace**
- Women in leadership face "女强人" (strong woman) label — respected but socially costly
- Strategy: Build relationship-based influence rather than authority-based
- Men may expect traditional deference from women colleagues — navigate carefully

**American tech workplace**
- DEI awareness is high but unconscious bias persists
- Strategy: Document contributions rigorously; use data-driven arguments
- Allyship networks are valuable — identify and cultivate them

**Korean/Japanese workplace**
- Gender hierarchy is more pronounced and explicit
- Strategy: Find sponsors in leadership who will advocate behind closed doors
- Formal channels may be more effective than direct confrontation

## When to Apply This Module

Activate the gender dynamics overlay when:
1. User mentions feeling that their behavior is being "read differently"
2. User is preparing for high-stakes conversations (raises, feedback, disagreements)
3. User's persona profile includes gender information
4. User is navigating a situation where research shows documented gender bias
5. User explicitly asks about gender-related communication challenges

**Do NOT** default to gender assumptions. Only apply when relevant data
is available or when the user raises the topic.
